Missing Planes and the Fragility of Progress

Contributors • Published on November 02, 2025

In this essay, we’ll dive into three legendary aviation mysteries: the disappearance of Amelia Earhart in 1937, the puzzling “STENDEC” signal from 1947, and the vanishing of Malaysia Airlines Flight MH370 in 2014. Each of these cases serves as a striking reminder that, despite our advanced technology—think satellites, radar, and predictive algorithms—we still can’t fully grasp every aspect of reality. These unsolved mysteries challenge the idea that we can know everything, showing us that sometimes, the unknown remains just that: unknown. Instead of seeing these mysteries as failures of modern life, they actually highlight its limitations. In a world that’s all about control and transparency, the unknown turns into a place where we can truly connect — a space where meaning can’t be neatly packaged, and our imagination gets to roam free. These gaps aren’t just empty spaces; they’re like mirrors that reflect the blind spots of our algorithm-driven era.

Impossible Images: Why are there photos from 1928 and 1943 that show people using mobile phones?

Contributors • Published on October 20, 2025

In this essay, we dive into the fascinating world of authentic archival photographs—think scenes like Chaplin’s The Circus (1928), a young person at the reopening of South Fork’s bridge (1941), and a mysterious figure in Reykjavík (1943). What’s intriguing is how these images seem to resonate with our modern lives, almost as if they’re showcasing mobile phones or other handheld devices. But don’t be fooled; these aren’t hoaxes. They’re real snapshots from the past. What’s happening here is that our contemporary cultural lens transforms subtle gestures—like someone holding a hand to their ear—into something that feels out of place in time. I suggest that these “impossible” moments don’t actually reside in the photographs themselves, but rather in how we interpret them. We’re projecting our current technology onto scenes from the past. This isn’t merely a case of nostalgia or trying to make sense of things; it’s a fascinating phenomenon called heterochrony, where different times overlap in surprising ways. In a world filled with deepfakes and algorithm-driven media, old photographs start to feel like “suspects.” They aren’t evidence of time travel, but rather prompts that challenge us to think about how we construct meaning. As modern viewers, we often impose our familiar gestures as proof and lean towards simplistic explanations. But there’s so much more to uncover if we dig a little deeper!

From Proportion to Perception: The Hidden Algorithm in Art

Contributors • Published on September 27, 2025

From Pythagoras's vibrating string to neural networks' layered vision, algorithms have always influenced art, first in sound and then in image. Painting followed suit, embedding divine proportions into space. The sectio aurea guided the hands of Leonardo and Botticelli as they composed visual symphonies with compass and ruler. In The Last Supper, Leonardo did more than depict a scene. He created a symbolic grid in which Christ becomes the center of both geometry and meaning. This brings to mind how attention mechanisms work in AI. Think of it like Schenkerian analysis in music, which uncovers deeper layers through a process of simplification. Similarly, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) break down images into different layers—like edges, shapes, textures, and meanings—much like artists from the Renaissance moved from rough sketches to full expressions. Both music and visual arts are fundamentally algorithmic; they have their own structure, layers, and symbolism. Today’s AI doesn’t disrupt tradition; it carries it forward. CNNs aren’t just tools; they’re the next generation in a long line of creative evolution.

From Dice to Algorithms: How the Story of Artificial Intelligence in Music Began

Contributors • Published on September 14, 2025

This article takes us on a fascinating journey through the history of musical AI, tracing its roots all the way back to the Enlightenment. It all kicks off with the Musikalisches Würfelspiel, a quirky dice game that created minuets by piecing together pre-composed measures based on random rolls and set tables. This clever concept was a precursor to what we now think of as compositional automation. From there, we dive into early mechanical systems like the Arca musarithmica, linking them to broader mathematical ideas, including Leibniz’s notion of mathesis universalis. The text shines a spotlight on Ada Lovelace, who made a groundbreaking realization: with the right instructions, a machine could generate intricate musical or even "scientific" compositions. What’s really important here is the argument that these early systems weren’t just simple chance-driven automatons. Instead, they were sophisticated combinatorial machines. Each musical fragment followed harmonic rules and formal structures, ensuring that even in the midst of randomness, the music remained coherent and engaging.

Voices, Tensions, and Harmonies in the Digital Age

Contributors • Published on July 19, 2025

In “Voices, Tensions, and Harmonies in the Digital Age,” the author contemplates a world where the future is no longer a distant projection but a dynamically unfolding score—line by line, decision by decision. Artificial intelligence, once a topic for futurists, emerges as a catalyst for deep reflections on memory, creativity, and ethical responsibility, bringing together philosophers, engineers, artists, and tech thinkers into an evolving polyphonic dialogue. This conversation resembles a symphony of ideas, oscillating between enthused curiosity—viewing algorithms as extensions of human inspiration—and quieter notes of apprehension, questioning the implications for autonomy, identity, and collective memory. The author emphasizes that these diverse voices, whether consonant or dissonant, form essential contrapuntal layers in understanding the future: not as a monolithic narrative, but as a space of negotiated meaning shaped in real time. Ultimately, the essay invites us to listen carefully—not to extract a single melody from this intellectual ensemble, but to learn how to navigate complexity, discern what is demonstrable (scientific or humanistic), and celebrate the harmonious intersection of technology and humanity. In this ongoing concert, attentive reflection becomes as vital as technical mastery.

Digital Aura: On Uniqueness, Networks, and the Future of Culture,

Contributors • Published on July 06, 2025

In “Digital Aura: On Uniqueness, Networks, and the Future of Culture,” the essay begins with a meditation on authenticity in an age of infinite digital reproduction. While pre-industrial societies valued the singular, handcrafted object, and the industrial revolution sparked a crisis of originality through mass production, today’s digital realm erases physical uniqueness entirely—raising the urgent question: how can uniqueness exist when every file can be infinitely duplicated? The author then turns to NFTs as a technological and symbolic solution. By anchoring a unique cryptographic identifier to a digital asset on the blockchain, NFTs do not merely store a file—they guarantee its provenance, metadata, and transactional history. This creates a form of “digital aura,” a resacralization of cultural value not tied to material presence, but sustained through network structure and immutable code. Extending beyond art, the essay explores how NFTs can serve as verifiable digital certificates in fields like healthcare and education—securing patient records, academic achievements, and lifelong learning pathways. Smart contracts further embed creator rights into the network, enabling automated royalty distribution with transparency and fairness. Through NFTs, the value of culture migrates from its physical form into a decentralized network of authenticity, appreciation, and algorithmic justice.

Culture, the Cabinet, and Sealed Time

Contributors • Published on June 30, 2025

In “Culture, the Cabinet, and Sealed Time,” the author reframes blockchain not merely as a tool for cryptocurrency, but as a living infrastructure for collective memory. Analogized to a digital cabinet with numbered drawers, each file is sealed with a cryptographic stamp—immutable and visible to all. No one actor holds solitary power; instead, trust is distributed across a network of participants who collectively steward cultural heritage. Moving beyond secure storage, the essay explores how GLAM institutions—galleries, libraries, archives, museums—are evolving from gatekeepers of curated memory to platforms of shared cultural negotiation. In this new participatory model, curation is democratized: communities vote, validate, and choose what enters the collective archive. Memory becomes a communal endeavor; culture not only preserved, but co‑authored, in real time. The deeper reflection ties blockchain’s architecture to philosophical concerns: echoes of Cartesian doubt meet algorithmic certainty. Like sealed capsules of time, blockchain entries carry the weight of communal values, forming what the essay positions as an “ecology of memory.” Here, authenticity is algorithmically guaranteed, and cultural significance is openly negotiated—ushering a new era where culture is living, participatory, and continuously reconfigured.
