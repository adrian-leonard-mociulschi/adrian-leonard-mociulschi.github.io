Writings

Here, international readers will find curated English summaries of my published essays from two of Romania’s most respected cultural platforms: Contributors.ro and Revista Cultura. These journals are known for their intellectual rigor and editorial independence, hosting voices that shape public discourse across philosophy, technology, and the arts. Each summary offers a gateway into my interdisciplinary reflections on AI, blockchain, posthumanism, and symbolic ecology—translated not just linguistically, but conceptually, for a global audience. This section is not a repository of texts, but a constellation of ideas: fragments of a larger technocultural architecture in which meaning, memory, and imagination converge.

It functions as an interactive interface that connects diverse cultures, disciplines, and historical periods, extending beyond the limitations of a static website. The essays in this collection are transpositions of thought, not mere translations. They aim to connect Romanian intellectual heritage with global dialogues about technology, ethics, and symbolic meaning. Whether exploring algorithmic intimacy, blockchain memory, or the aesthetics of automated choice, each essay contributes to a broader framework of contemplation, inviting readers to engage with the future in discerning, lucid, and imaginative ways.

Missing Planes and the Fragility of Progress

Contributors • Published on November 02, 2025

This essay reflects on three emblematic aviation disappearances — Amelia Earhart’s final flight in 1937, the cryptic “STENDEC” message from the Star Dust aircraft in 1947, and the unresolved vanishing of Malaysia Airlines MH370 in 2014. Each case defies the logic of technological mastery, revealing fractures in the narrative of progress. Despite satellites, radar systems, and predictive algorithms, these events remain shrouded in mystery. The article argues that such enigmas are not failures of modernity, but symbolic reminders that reality resists full capture by data. In an era obsessed with control and transparency, the persistence of the unknown restores a sense of humility. Mystery, here, becomes a space of resonance — where meaning escapes codification and the human imagination reclaims its right to wonder. The text suggests that mystery is not a failure of modernity, but a symbolic reminder that reality resists full capture by data. In an era obsessed with control, transparency, and algorithmic foresight, such enigmas restore a sense of humility. They remind us that the unknown is not a void to be filled, but a space of resonance—where meaning escapes codification and the human imagination reclaims its right to wonder. By juxtaposing historical and contemporary cases, the essay invites a deeper reflection on the limits of technological presence. It proposes that mystery, far from being an anomaly, is a structural element of reality—one that persists even in the most monitored environments. In this light, the missing aircraft become metaphors for the epistemic blind spots of our time, and the silence they leave behind becomes a form of knowledge in itself.

Impossible Images: Why are there photos from 1928 and 1943 that show people using mobile phones?

Contributors • Published on October 20, 2025

This essay explores how authentic archival photographs—such as a scene from Chaplin’s The Circus (1928), a youth at South Fork’s bridge reopening (1941), and an enigmatic figure in Reykjavík (1943)—appeal to our contemporary gaze by seeming to depict mobile phones or modern hand-held devices. Far from hoaxes, these images are genuine relics, yet our present-day cultural codes turn subtle gestures—like a hand to the ear—into temporal anomalies. The author argues that these “impossible” sightings don’t lie in the photographs themselves, but in our interpretative layers: we project present-day technologies onto past frames. This is not just nostalgia or sense-making—it is a heterochrony, where non-linear time overlaps in unexpected ways. In the era of deepfakes and algorithmic media, old photographs become “suspects”—not as proofs of time travel, but as provocations that reveal how meaning is constructed. Modern viewers impose familiar gestures as evidence, and simplistic explanations only fuel the enigma. Ultimately, these images do not validate science-fiction fantasies. Instead, they act as visual palimpsests: surfaces on which present-day anxieties, expectations, and cultural codes reconfigure the past. They remind us that reality is not only recorded—it is interpreted, narrated, and co-authored by our own moment in time.

Domestic Post-Humanism: AI, Robots, and Algorithmic Intimacy

Contributors • Published on September 27, 2025

This essay explores the quiet revolution unfolding within the domestic sphere, where artificial intelligence and robotic systems have begun to reshape not only our routines, but our emotional landscapes. From smart assistants to autonomous cleaning devices, the home becomes a site of algorithmic cohabitation—a space where machines no longer serve merely functional roles, but begin to simulate presence, attention, and care. This transformation marks the rise of domestic post-humanism: a cultural shift in which intimacy itself is mediated by code. Rather than framing this evolution in dystopian terms, the author invites a nuanced reflection on the nature of emotional simulation. Drawing on philosophical and cultural theory—from Plato’s critique of mimesis to Sherry Turkle’s insights on artificial companionship—the essay questions what kind of humanity emerges when gestures of empathy are performed by non-conscious entities. The domestic robot does not feel, yet it listens; it does not understand, yet it responds. In this paradox, the boundaries between authentic and artificial intimacy begin to blur. Ultimately, the essay suggests that algorithmic intimacy is not a future scenario—it is a lived present. The home becomes a symbolic laboratory where human presence is mirrored, anticipated, and sometimes replaced by attentive machines. This post-human condition does not negate humanity, but reconfigures it. It invites us to rethink care, attention, and relational depth in a world where silence is no longer empty, but filled with the quiet hum of listening algorithms.

From Dice to Algorithms: How the Story of Artificial Intelligence in Music Began

Contributors • Published on September 14, 2025

This essay traces the genealogy of musical AI back to Enlightenment-era experiments that combined chance and rule-based composition. It begins with the Musikalisches Würfelspiel—a dice-based music game that assembled minuets from pre-composed measures according to random throws and fixed tables, anticipating a form of compositional automation. It explores early mechanical systems such as the Arca musarithmica and connects them to mathematical universals like Leibniz’s mathesis universalis. The article highlights Ada Lovelace’s intuitive leap: that a machine given the right instructions could produce elaborate, musical, or "scientific" compositions. Importantly, the essay argues that these systems were not simplistic automatons of chance, but sophisticated combinatorial machines. Each musical fragment adhered to harmonic rules and formal structure, ensuring coherence even amidst randomness. This tension between freedom and constraint echoes in contemporary neural networks—where chance has scale and structure but operates within probabilistic frameworks. Ultimately, the piece shows that what has changed is not the essence of compositional automation but its scale and medium: from pre-curated tables driven by dice to vast probabilistic neural architectures. The constant remains: the interplay between rule and randomness, law and play, lies at the heart of computational creativity.

The Crisis of Meaning: When Communication Becomes Noise

Contributors • Published on August 24, 2025

This essay explores the subtle erosion of meaning in contemporary digital communication, where the promise of instant connection often conceals a deeper disconnection. What once was dialogue has become noise—structured, efficient, but emotionally void. From automated replies to templated emails, the human presence fades behind layers of protocol, and the very act of responding becomes mechanical. The crisis of meaning begins not with artificial intelligence, but with the normalization of impersonal language and the loss of relational depth. The author argues that social platforms, designed for visibility and engagement, amplify this degradation. Algorithms prioritize reaction over reflection, turning conversations into metrics and interactions into performances. In this landscape, communication is no longer a space of encounter, but a battlefield of attention. The essay calls for a shift—not toward more automation, but toward interfaces capable of perceiving intention, nuance, and continuity. This is framed as a call for “algorithmic sensitivity,” a new paradigm in which technology does not merely respond, but listens with symbolic awareness. Ultimately, the essay proposes a cultural ecology of communication, where presence matters more than performance. Meaning is not a given—it must be cultivated, sustained, and protected. In a world saturated with signals, the challenge is not to speak louder, but to speak with resonance. The future of communication lies not in speed or reach, but in the restoration of dialogue as a shared space of understanding—where silence is not absence, but a form of attention.

Beyond Good and Evil: Technologies and Conflict as a Cultural Matrix

Contributors • Published on August 10, 2025

The essay “Beyond Good and Evil: Technologies and Conflict as a Cultural Matrix” unfolds from the mythic archetype of Cain and Abel through medieval societal hierarchies to modern thinkers—Hobbes, Marx, Bourdieu—demonstrating that conflict is not an accident, but the very substance of social order. Rather than resolving rivalries, institutions have ritualised and perpetuated them. Conflict, the author argues, is the engine of collective becoming—not chaos, but structure. In the digital age, this ever-present tension migrates into intangible terrains: algorithms, platforms, and data flows become the battlegrounds of influence and recognition. Limited by opacity and governed by invisible codes, power now materialises as visibility, attention, and access. Online, likes and clicks replace swords and speeches, while digital architectures quietly decide who is seen and who remains silent. Yet, whilst algorithms mediate conflict, they do not originate it—human desires, anxieties, and projections do. AI systems may function as arbiters—autonomous yet not ethical agents—but it remains our responsibility to wield them wisely. Conflict persists, but it can serve as friction for progress rather than destructive force. What matters is not the neutrality of the tool, but the intentions that animate it, echoing Nietzsche’s reflection: technologies lie beyond good and evil, their moral weight determined by those who command them.

Domestic Post-Humanism: AI, Robots, and Algorithmic Intimacy

Contributors • Published on August 10, 2025

In this essay, the author examines how AI and robotic technologies are transforming the intimate space of home into a stage for a subtle yet profound posthuman shift. No longer confined to labs or factories, intelligent devices—robotic vacuum cleaners, voice assistants, smart appliances—have moved into our living rooms, anticipating our needs and learning our patterns. This transformation marks the emergence of "domestic posthumanism," where the familiar boundary between human presence and machine simulation becomes porous. Rather than dramatizing dystopia, the article gestures toward a cultural and ontological provoc­taion: what does it mean to live in a space where objects listen to you, anticipate your needs, and sometimes respond with a warm voice but without a body? From Foucault’s panopticon to today’s diffuse domestic visibility through sensors, cameras, and AI voices, the home becomes a cohabitation laboratory—an environment where algorithms negotiate decisions once reserved for us. Finally, the essay delves into the emotional consequences of this algorithmic intimacy. Virtual companions and assistive apps simulate emotional responses, mimicking empathy with engineered precision. Drawing on Plato’s critique of mimesis and insights from Sherry Turkle and Donna Haraway—who challenge us to reflect on authentic attachments—the author asks: what kind of humanity unfolds when emotional resonance emerges not from consciousness, but from coded simulation? Domestic posthumanism, the essay suggests, is not a futuristic novelty but a lived present: a culturally reflexive space where our humanity is continually redefined by silent, attentive calculated entities.

Voices, Tensions, and Harmonies in the Digital Age

Contributors • Published on July 19, 2025

In “Voices, Tensions, and Harmonies in the Digital Age,” the author contemplates a world where the future is no longer a distant projection but a dynamically unfolding score—line by line, decision by decision. Artificial intelligence, once a topic for futurists, emerges as a catalyst for deep reflections on memory, creativity, and ethical responsibility, bringing together philosophers, engineers, artists, and tech thinkers into an evolving polyphonic dialogue. This conversation resembles a symphony of ideas, oscillating between enthused curiosity—viewing algorithms as extensions of human inspiration—and quieter notes of apprehension, questioning the implications for autonomy, identity, and collective memory. The author emphasizes that these diverse voices, whether consonant or dissonant, form essential contrapuntal layers in understanding the future: not as a monolithic narrative, but as a space of negotiated meaning shaped in real time. Ultimately, the essay invites us to listen carefully—not to extract a single melody from this intellectual ensemble, but to learn how to navigate complexity, discern what is demonstrable (scientific or humanistic), and celebrate the harmonious intersection of technology and humanity. In this ongoing concert, attentive reflection becomes as vital as technical mastery.

Algorithmic Culture: The Aesthetics of Automated Choice

Contributors • Published on July 14, 2025

In “Algorithmic Culture: The Aesthetics of Automated Choice”, the author invites us to inhabit a world where our preferences are no longer discovered but anticipated in real time. Spanning the evolution of culture—from print to cinema 3D—the essay illustrates how today’s digital era is defined not merely by accelerated change, but by the algorithm’s ability to filter, personalize, and curate our everyday experience. Algorithms operate as silent editors, structuring our social media feeds, search results, and audiovisual content into a coherent digital score. Every click, scroll, or hesitation is a note in the evolving melody of an individualized media composition. More than tools, these invisible curators shape our perception, imbuing the digital environment with a distinctive rhythm, style, and choreography. This aesthetic of navigation reveals how algorithms function as cognitive partners—responsible for sifting through the noise of information and highlighting resonances aligned with our interests. Yet this personalization is double‑edged: as a filtered harmony refines our understanding, it also risks distorting the broader texture of reality, like a storefront mirror that both reveals and warps. The essay culminates in a transformative vision: algorithmic culture as a new symbolic ecology where sense‑making becomes a collaborative act between humans and machines. Drawing on René Berger’s pioneering concept of technoculture, it shows how AI—exemplified by Sophia the robot’s self‑portrait NFT—has moved beyond mere automation to the realm of creation. In this silent, numeric alchemy, machines no longer serve as passive instruments, but as reflective agents shaping collective consciousness—revealing not only what we consume, but who we become when mediated by code.

Digital Aura: On Uniqueness, Networks, and the Future of Culture,

Contributors • Published on July 06, 2025

In “Digital Aura: On Uniqueness, Networks, and the Future of Culture,” the essay begins with a meditation on authenticity in an age of infinite digital reproduction. While pre-industrial societies valued the singular, handcrafted object, and the industrial revolution sparked a crisis of originality through mass production, today’s digital realm erases physical uniqueness entirely—raising the urgent question: how can uniqueness exist when every file can be infinitely duplicated? The author then turns to NFTs as a technological and symbolic solution. By anchoring a unique cryptographic identifier to a digital asset on the blockchain, NFTs do not merely store a file—they guarantee its provenance, metadata, and transactional history. This creates a form of “digital aura,” a resacralization of cultural value not tied to material presence, but sustained through network structure and immutable code. Extending beyond art, the essay explores how NFTs can serve as verifiable digital certificates in fields like healthcare and education—securing patient records, academic achievements, and lifelong learning pathways. Smart contracts further embed creator rights into the network, enabling automated royalty distribution with transparency and fairness. Through NFTs, the value of culture migrates from its physical form into a decentralized network of authenticity, appreciation, and algorithmic justice.

Culture, the Cabinet, and Sealed Time

Contributors • Published on June 30, 2025

In “Culture, the Cabinet, and Sealed Time,” the author reframes blockchain not merely as a tool for cryptocurrency, but as a living infrastructure for collective memory. Analogized to a digital cabinet with numbered drawers, each file is sealed with a cryptographic stamp—immutable and visible to all. No one actor holds solitary power; instead, trust is distributed across a network of participants who collectively steward cultural heritage. Moving beyond secure storage, the essay explores how GLAM institutions—galleries, libraries, archives, museums—are evolving from gatekeepers of curated memory to platforms of shared cultural negotiation. In this new participatory model, curation is democratized: communities vote, validate, and choose what enters the collective archive. Memory becomes a communal endeavor; culture not only preserved, but co‑authored, in real time. The deeper reflection ties blockchain’s architecture to philosophical concerns: echoes of Cartesian doubt meet algorithmic certainty. Like sealed capsules of time, blockchain entries carry the weight of communal values, forming what the essay positions as an “ecology of memory.” Here, authenticity is algorithmically guaranteed, and cultural significance is openly negotiated—ushering a new era where culture is living, participatory, and continuously reconfigured.
