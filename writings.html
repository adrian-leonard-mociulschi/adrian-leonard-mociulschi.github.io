Welcome to my Writings section!

This page brings together a curated selection of my published essays from Contributors.ro and Nine O’Clock, presented through concise summaries that offer clear entry points into my work on AI, technoculture, civic meaning and contemporary cultural dynamics. 🔗 From Particles to Algorithms: How a 'Demon' Determines What Becomes Visible in the Universe and on the Internet

Contributors • Published on February 21, 2026

The essay reframes Maxwell’s demon as a metaphor for how algorithms filter today’s information flows, shaping what becomes visible or invisible online. Moving from thermodynamic to digital entropy, it links Boltzmann’s statistical laws and Xenakis’s Pithoprakta to the probabilistic dynamics that govern viral content and attention. AI appears as a modern ordering demon, reducing informational chaos and amplifying oscillations between structure and noise—like a Pythagorean string that generates stability through vibration. The final reflection connects this demon to the Gnostic Demiurge: a technical organizer of worlds that can sort reality without granting it meaning. The text argues that the real task belongs to humans—transforming algorithmic order into resonance and sense-making.

🔗 Sacred Modernism, East of Vienna: From Niculescu’s Inner Liturgy to Łukaszewski’s Luminous Precision

Nine O'Clock • Published on February 13, 2026

This article explores the shared aesthetic lineage between Ștefan Niculescu and Paweł Łukaszewski, two major figures of European spiritual modernism. It examines how their music reshapes sacred sound through structural clarity, luminous restraint, and an architecture of interiority. Positioning Niculescu’s inner liturgy and Łukaszewski’s disciplined radiance along a Bucharest–Warsaw axis, the text argues that both composers advance a form of sacred modernism in which silence, proportion, and structural coherence become the true vehicles of spiritual expression in 21st‑century music.

🔗 What Happens When the Planet Reminds Us Who's in Charge?

🔗 From WWII to NATO: The Enduring Role of Romania’s Mountain Troops

🔗 New Year's Eve Under the Stars: How Music and Astronomy Are Writing the Future Together

Contributors • Published on December 31, 2025

What if the universe were not only something we see, but something we hear? This essay takes you on a journey where music and astronomy meet across millennia. From Pythagoras’ mystical harmony of the spheres and Boethius’ cosmic ratios to Kepler’s celestial counterpoint, the text traces how humanity has always imagined the stars as a score waiting to be played. Fast-forward to today: the James Webb Space Telescope doesn’t just capture light—it translates it into sound through sonification projects, turning data into melodies that echo the ancient dream of a singing cosmos. Along the way, we encounter the Antikythera mechanism, a relic of Greek ingenuity, and modern algorithms that act like discreet composers, shaping patterns hidden in the vastness of space. This is not mere nostalgia; it’s a vision of the future where science and art co-create meaning. My essay argues that technology does not erase poetry—it amplifies it, giving us new ways to inhabit the universe with imagination and precision. In an age obsessed with prediction and control, my text invites us to pause and listen: to hear the resonance between proportion and freedom, between the measurable and the ineffable.

🔗 Pythagoras and the Music of the Spheres. Sound and mathematics

Contributors • Published on December 20, 2025

From Pythagoras’s simple monochord to today’s complex neural networks, this piece takes us on an intriguing journey through history. It shows how harmonic ratios laid the groundwork for the very first algorithms, embedding a kind of cultural DNA that continues to shape our digital world. We’ll dive into the surprising connections between ancient music theory and modern AI, uncovering why the logic of sound is more than just beautiful—it’s also deeply computational. In a time when everyone seems fixated on predictions, this essay makes a case for resonance, presenting a rich landscape where meaning and technology come together in harmony; it’s really about the cultural frameworks that help us make sense of that data. It’s like creating a rich ecosystem of meaning where algorithms don’t just take over art—they actually enhance it. If coding is the language we all speak today, then harmony is like the original blueprint, blending math, emotion, and design into something beautiful. So, let’s challenge ourselves to see technology as a symbolic practice—one that can shape our future with clarity, ethics, and a touch of beauty.

🔗 Missing Planes and the Fragility of Progress

Contributors • Published on November 02, 2025

This essay explores three notable aviation enigmas: the 1937 disappearance of Amelia Earhart, the enigmatic “STENDEC” signal from 1947, and the vanishing of Malaysia Airlines Flight MH370 in 2014. Each case serves as a compelling reminder that, despite our sophisticated technology—such as satellites, radar, and predictive algorithms—we cannot fully comprehend every facet of reality. These unresolved mysteries challenge the notion of complete knowledge, illustrating that the unknown often remains just that: unknown. Rather than viewing these mysteries as shortcomings of contemporary life, they emphasize its inherent limitations. In a society focused on control and transparency, the unexpected becomes a realm for genuine connection—a domain where meaning cannot be easily defined, allowing our imagination to explore freely. These voids are not mere absences; they act as reflections of the blind spots inherent in our algorithm-driven age.

🔗 Impossible Images: Why are there photos from 1928 and 1943 that show people using mobile phones?

Contributors • Published on October 20, 2025

In this essay, we explore the compelling realm of authentic archival photographs—consider scenes such as Chaplin’s The Circus (1928), a young individual at the reopening of South Fork’s bridge (1941), and an enigmatic figure in Reykjavík (1943). What is particularly noteworthy is how these images appear to resonate with contemporary life, almost as if they are depicting mobile phones or other handheld devices. However, these are not fabrications; they are genuine snapshots from history. The phenomenon at play is that our modern cultural perspective reinterprets subtle actions—such as an individual cupping their hand to their ear—into something that seems temporally dissonant. I propose that these “impossible” moments do not inherently exist within the photographs, but rather in our interpretation of them. We are projecting our current technological context onto historical scenes. This is not simply a matter of nostalgia or an attempt to rationalize the past; it represents a captivating phenomenon known as heterochrony, where different times overlap in surprising ways.

🔗 From Proportion to Perception: The Hidden Algorithm in Art

Contributors • Published on October 05, 2025

From the vibrating string of Pythagoras to the layered vision of neural networks, algorithms have consistently shaped art, initially in sound and subsequently in visual form. Painting followed this trajectory, incorporating divine proportions into spatial compositions. The sectio aurea influenced the artistry of Leonardo and Botticelli as they crafted visual symphonies using compass and ruler. In The Last Supper, Leonardo transcended mere depiction; he established a symbolic grid where Christ occupies the nexus of geometry and meaning. This concept parallels the functioning of attention mechanisms in AI. Consider it akin to Schenkerian analysis in music, which reveals deeper layers through simplification. Likewise, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) deconstruct images into various layers—such as edges, shapes, textures, and meanings—mirroring how Renaissance artists progressed from initial sketches to complete works. Both music and visual arts are inherently algorithmic; they possess their own structured methodologies.

🔗 From Dice to Algorithms: How the Story of Artificial Intelligence in Music Began

Contributors • Published on September 14, 2025

This article embarks on an insightful exploration of the history of musical AI, tracing its origins back to the Enlightenment. It begins with the Musikalisches Würfelspiel, an innovative dice game that generated minuets by assembling pre-composed measures based on random rolls and predetermined tables. This ingenious concept served as a precursor to contemporary notions of compositional automation. Subsequently, we examine early mechanical systems such as the Arca musarithmica, connecting them to broader mathematical concepts, including Leibniz’s idea of mathesis universalis. The discussion highlights Ada Lovelace, who made a pivotal discovery: with appropriate instructions, a machine could produce complex musical or even "scientific" compositions. Crucially, the argument presented is that these early systems were not merely chance-driven automatons; rather, they were advanced combinatorial machines.

🔗 The Crisis of Meaning: When Communication Becomes Noise

Contributors • Published on August 24, 2025

This essay examines the nuanced ways in which our digital communication is diminishing in significance. Although we are promised immediate connections, we frequently experience a profound sense of disconnection. Genuine conversations have transformed into mere noise—while they may be structured and efficient, they often lack true emotional resonance. Consider the prevalence of automated responses and standardized emails; the human element appears to be obscured by layers of protocol, rendering our interactions more mechanical than sincere. The fundamental crisis of meaning does not originate with artificial intelligence; it arises from our acceptance of impersonal language and the erosion of the rich relational depth we once enjoyed. In this digital environment, communication resembles less a welcoming exchange and more a competitive arena for our attention. What is truly needed is a transition—not towards increased automation, but towards technology that genuinely comprehends our intentions and acknowledges nuances.

🔗 Beyond Good and Evil: Technologies and Conflict as a Cultural Matrix

Contributors • Published on August 10, 2025

🔗 Digital Aura: On Uniqueness, Networks, and the Future of Culture,

Contributors • Published on July 06, 2025

This piece opens with a reflection on authenticity amidst the era of limitless digital reproduction. While pre-industrial societies esteemed the unique, handcrafted item, the industrial revolution initiated a crisis of originality due to mass production. In contrast, the contemporary digital landscape completely diminishes physical uniqueness, prompting the critical inquiry: how can uniqueness persist when every file is subject to infinite duplication? The author subsequently examines NFTs as both a technological and symbolic remedy. By attaching a unique cryptographic identifier to a digital asset on the blockchain, NFTs serve not only to store a file but also to ensure its provenance, metadata, and transactional history. This results in a form of "digital aura," a resacralization of cultural value that is not dependent on material presence but is upheld through network architecture and immutable code. The discussion extends beyond art, as the essay investigates how this "aura" redefines cultural legitimacy in a decentralized world — where value is no longer conferred by institutional gatekeepers, but emerges from symbolic consensus, cryptographic trust, and the performative presence of the network itself.

🔗 Culture, Wardrobe, and Sealed Time

Contributors • Published on June 30, 2025

The "Culture, the Cabinet, and Sealed Time," try to reframe blockchain not merely as a tool for cryptocurrency, but as a living infrastructure for collective memory. Analogized to a digital cabinet with numbered drawers, each file is sealed with a cryptographic stamp—immutable and visible to all. No one actor holds solitary power; instead, trust is distributed across a network of participants who collectively steward cultural heritage. Moving beyond secure storage, the essay explores how GLAM institutions—galleries, libraries, archives, museums—are evolving from gatekeepers of curated memory to platforms of shared cultural negotiation. In this new participatory model, curation is democratized: communities vote, validate, and choose what enters the collective archive. Memory becomes a communal endeavor; culture not only preserved, but co‑authored, in real time. The deeper reflection ties blockchain’s architecture to philosophical concerns: echoes of Cartesian doubt meet algorithmic certainty. Like sealed capsules of time, blockchain entries carry the weight of communal values, forming what the essay positions as an "ecology of memory." Here, authenticity is algorithmically guaranteed, and cultural significance is openly negotiated—ushering a new era where culture is living, participatory, and continuously reconfigured.
