Missing Planes and the Fragility of Progress

Contributors • Published on November 02, 2025

This essay reflects on three emblematic aviation disappearances — Amelia Earhart’s final flight in 1937, the cryptic “STENDEC” message from the Star Dust aircraft in 1947, and the unresolved vanishing of Malaysia Airlines MH370 in 2014. Each case defies the logic of technological mastery, revealing fractures in the narrative of progress. Despite satellites, radar systems, and predictive algorithms, these events remain shrouded in mystery. The article argues that such enigmas are not failures of modernity, but symbolic reminders that reality resists full capture by data. In an era obsessed with control and transparency, the persistence of the unknown restores a sense of humility. Mystery, here, becomes a space of resonance — where meaning escapes codification and the human imagination reclaims its right to wonder.

Impossible Images: Why are there photos from 1928 and 1943 that show people using mobile phones?

Contributors • Published on October 20, 2025

This essay explores how authentic archival photographs—such as a scene from Chaplin’s The Circus (1928), a youth at South Fork’s bridge reopening (1941), and an enigmatic figure in Reykjavík (1943)—appeal to our contemporary gaze by seeming to depict mobile phones or modern hand-held devices. Far from hoaxes, these images are genuine relics, yet our present-day cultural codes turn subtle gestures—like a hand to the ear—into temporal anomalies. The author argues that these “impossible” sightings don’t lie in the photographs themselves, but in our interpretative layers: we project present-day technologies onto past frames. This is not just nostalgia or sense-making—it is a heterochrony, where non-linear time overlaps in unexpected ways. In the era of deepfakes and algorithmic media, old photographs become “suspects”—not as proofs of time travel, but as provocations that reveal how meaning is constructed. Modern viewers impose familiar gestures as evidence, and simplistic explanations only fuel the enigma. Ultimately, these images do not validate science-fiction fantasies. Instead, they act as visual palimpsests: surfaces on which present-day anxieties, expectations, and cultural codes reconfigure the past. They remind us that reality is not only recorded—it is interpreted, narrated, and co-authored by our own moment in time.

The Murmur of Code: On the Ethics of Algorithmic Presence

Contributors • Published on September 27, 2025

This essay traces the ancient roots of algorithmic thinking from temporal to spatial arts—from music’s numerical harmony to the geometric logic of Renaissance painting. It begins by recalling Pythagoras’s discovery of musical intervals and the Fourier analysis of sound, showing how harmony is algorithmic in nature. It then moves to visual arts, arguing that artists like Leonardo da Vinci and Dürer embedded hidden proportional grids—Greek and golden ratios—that anticipate modern convolutional neural networks (CNNs), which decompose images through layered feature extraction. Ultimately, the essay proposes that the algorithm is not a modern intrusion into art, but a latent thread linking ancient compositional practices with contemporary AI systems. It reframes art, music, neural networks, and even blockchain as points on a continuous topology of meaning—an emerging technocultural ecology where algorithmic vision and human insight co-construct our experience of reality.
